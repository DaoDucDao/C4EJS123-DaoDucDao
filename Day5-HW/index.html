What Mary Oliver's Critics Don't Understand
For America's most beloved poet, paying attention to nature is a springboat to the sacred.

By Ruth Franklin



Oliver uses nature as a springboat to the scared - the beating heart of her work.

Illustration by Deanna Halsall

"Mary Oliver is saving my life", Paul Chowder, the title character of Nicholson baker's novel " The Anthologist" scrawls in the margins of Oliver's "New and Selected Poems, Volume One." A struggling poet, Chowder is suffering from a severe case of writer's block. His girlfriend, with whome he's lived for eight years, has just left him, ostensible because he has been unable to write the long-overdue introduction to a poetry anthology that he has been putting together. For solace in inspiration, he turns to poets who have been his touchstones - LOuise Bogan, Theodore Roetheke, Sara Teadale - before discovering Oliver. In her work, he finds consolation: "I immediately felt more sure of what I was doing." Of her poems, he says, "They're very simple. And yet each has something."

I don't know exacly what a prayer is.
I do know how to pay attention, how to fall down
the grass, how to kneel down in the grass,
to be idle and blessed, how to stroll through the fields,
is what I have been doing all day.

The cadences are almost Biblical. "Attention is the beginning of devotion," She urges elsewhere.
